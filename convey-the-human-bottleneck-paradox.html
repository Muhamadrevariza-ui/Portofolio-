Prologue

CONVEY

The Human Bottleneck Paradox

Convey promises a new model of enterprise automation: instead of employees manually performing repetitive workflows, non-technical operators show the work, and Convey creates digital teammates capable of executing those workflows across enterprise software.

What We Know

The promise is speed and scale. But as we peel back the layers of this operational model, a deeper question emerges. It is not whether the AI can do the work.

"What infrastructure is required to scale digital labor, and can that infrastructure itself scale efficiently?"

This investigation does not assume the answer is known. It examines the structural requirements of scaling automation beyond the initial deployment.

Explanatory Model — Not Empirical Evidence

The Factory Robot

Imagine replacing 100 factory workers with 100 robots. Productivity spikes initially. But the factory soon discovers it needs technicians to install them, engineers to connect them, and specialists to repair them when they jam.

Scaling automation requires more than just the agents. It requires a supporting infrastructure to keep them running.

To understand if Convey has built this infrastructure, we must first examine how its technology differs from raw AI models.

Act III

The Scaling Question — FDE Hypotheses

Public signals around Forward Deployed Engineers are analytically important but ambiguous. FDE hiring alone does not prove implementation complexity.

Supporting Logic

Enterprise integrations, security requirements, workflow mapping, exceptions, and customer-specific environments require substantial human implementation work.

Strategic Implication

Convey could face services-like scaling pressure if deployment effort grows proportionally with customers or revenue.

What Would Strengthen This

FDE headcount growing at a similar rate to customer count or revenue over multiple periods.

What Would Weaken This

Evidence that deployment time per customer is shrinking as the platform matures.

Supporting Logic

FDE growth may represent temporary human bridge-building during rapid demand growth. Engineers may be creating reusable deployment infrastructure, templates, abstractions, and product improvements.

Strategic Implication

Human deployment effort per customer could decline as the platform matures.

What Would Strengthen This

Evidence of reusable templates, faster onboarding cycles, or declining FDE-to-customer ratios over time.

What Would Weaken This

Continued proportional FDE hiring alongside customer growth with no efficiency gains.

The Missing Metric

FDE Headcount Growth vs. ARR Growth

If ARR grows substantially faster than FDE headcount, this would support improving deployment leverage. If FDE headcount and ARR scale approximately together, this could suggest services-like scaling pressure.

If someone relies on GPS for years, they may gradually stop remembering routes. When navigation fails in an unfamiliar situation, recovery becomes harder precisely because routine participation has declined.

Strategic Hypothesis

The Human Bottleneck Paradox

As digital labor scales, the human role may shift away from direct execution toward implementation, supervision, maintenance, intervention, and recovery. The strategic question is whether this control infrastructure can scale faster than the complexity it is designed to manage.

Explanatory Model — Not Empirical Evidence

The Manager of 100 Digital Employees

Imagine one manager supervising 100 digital employees. They work quickly and continuously. But when one fails, the manager must diagnose why, even though they may not have manually performed that workflow in months.

The AI scaled. Did the management infrastructure?

So, what is the verdict? Can Convey solve the paradox it has identified?

Epilogue

Strategic Verdict & The Control Infrastructure Opportunity

What We Know

Public evidence suggests Convey has created meaningful customer value in documented deployments.

What The Evidence Suggests

Convey may have identified an important strategic layer between increasingly capable AI models and reliable enterprise execution.

What We Still Cannot Know

The decisive economic question remains unresolved: can digital workforce deployment scale materially faster than the human infrastructure required to implement, supervise, maintain, and recover it?

Strategic Hypothesis

A strategically plausible opportunity exists around becoming increasingly important control infrastructure for digital labor. This is a possibility. Not a guaranteed outcome.

Appendix

Methodology, Evidence Standards, Limitations & Sources

Methodology

Hypothesis → Evidence → Analysis → Alternative Explanations → Missing Evidence → Strategic Verdict.

Evidence Standards

  • Verified Fact — supported by credible public evidence.
  • Reasonable Inference — a plausible reading of available evidence.
  • Strategic Hypothesis — a proposition under investigation, not established.
  • Competing Hypotheses — multiple plausible explanations evidence cannot distinguish.
  • Explanatory Model — an analogy used only to explain a mechanism.
  • Missing Evidence — an unresolved question, explicitly marked.

Limitations

  • Internal unit economics unavailable
  • FDE / ARR ratio unavailable
  • Long-term maintenance data unavailable
  • Exception rates unavailable
  • Internal architecture details unavailable
  • Public case studies may not represent typical deployments
