A Business Investigation

A business already running at 75 has fresh capital and a short runway to reach 100.

Fresh capital doesn't answer the question that actually decides the sprint: which of the business's growth engines can turn that capital into pace. Supply6, an Indian D2C wellness company, is now facing a version of this problem with real capital, a near-term target, and several possible engines — and the unresolved question is not whether more growth engines exist, but what public evidence can actually tell us about their relative readiness.

Supply6 has multiple publicly observable growth engines — flagship products, repeat buyers, marketplaces, quick commerce, new launches, international plans. 03

The Evidence Asymmetry

Are Supply6's growth directions equally visible to an outside investigator?

Supply6 has several publicly visible growth directions. But they do not leave equally measurable evidence. Some leave a real public trail — listings, ratings, reviews, prices. Others exist mainly as reported expansion intentions, without an individually verifiable public dataset behind them.

This is a statement about observability, not about business quality. An engine appearing more observable below does not mean it is economically stronger, more profitable, or more deserving of capital — only that more of its activity happens to leave a public trace an outside investigator can find.

Retained, individually attributable public evidence

Four growth directions fall into this group, but not on equal footing: three are supported by directly observable primary traces — marketplace listings and official product pages. The fourth, Repeat Purchase, rests on a metric attributable to specifically named public reporting, not a directly observable dataset. Both are individually traceable to a named source; neither is treated here as equally strong evidence.

04

The Readiness Gap

Why can't these growth engines be compared consistently?

Screen 03 showed that Supply6 has more than one visible growth option. That could make this look like a simple question: which one is strongest? Laid out side by side, though, each engine turns out to be visible through a different type of evidence — engagement data for one, a self-reported rate for another, geographic presence for a third. None of those measures convert into the others.

Imagine six roads leading toward the same destination. Public evidence can show which roads exist and which have been travelled before — but not the cost of taking each one faster. The six roads are the observable growth engines above; the missing cost comparison is the relative economics needed to evaluate them on one basis.

The problem is not that no data exists for these engines. It's that different engines are visible through different types of data — which is why they cannot be ranked against each other on one consistent basis from public evidence alone.

What this classification measures — and what it does not

Classification measures public observability and evidence strength — not economic attractiveness, expected ROI, or recommended capital allocation.

Limited

Evidence is primarily indirect, incomplete, highly dynamic, or supported by very few observable data points.

Medium

Multiple relevant public evidence points exist, but important variables remain unavailable or non-comparable.

Medium–High

Multiple credible evidence points consistently support the mechanism, while material internal economic data is still unavailable.

Near-term relevance

Uncertain, Medium, or High reflects how directly an engine's public evidence connects to the 3–4 month, 33.3% target — not a prediction of outcome.

None of the categories above say which engine will generate the highest return, which deserves the most capital, or which management should prioritize. They describe what public evidence can show — not what it's worth.

05

The Decision Bottleneck

What would management need to know before accelerating one engine over another?

What public evidence can show

  • The growth target and timeframe
  • The funding event
  • Historical growth signals
  • Product architecture
  • Reported repeat-purchase rate
  • Observable customer engagement
  • Channel presence
  • Publicly stated expansion priorities

What public evidence cannot show

  • Revenue by SKU
  • Contribution margin by product
  • Repeat rate by cohort
  • CAC by channel
  • Customer lifetime value
  • Marketplace contribution margin
  • Quick-commerce sales velocity
  • Incremental return on capital

These are not generic "nice-to-have" internal metrics. Together, these metrics form a practical comparison set for evaluating the relative economics of the observable growth engines — the public-data gaps most directly blocking a consistent comparison across them, whether the decision is to back one engine or several at once.

The evidence that would resolve the decision

Conditional analytical logic — not a recommendation

IFflagship products show superior contribution economics
→
THENscaling the existing engine becomes more defensible
IFnewer products show accelerating repeat purchase and revenue ramp
→
THENportfolio expansion becomes more defensible
IFquick commerce delivers incremental customers at sustainable economics
→
THENchannel expansion becomes more defensible

Supply6 does not appear short of growth engines. What public evidence cannot establish is the relative economics across them — the comparison needed to allocate capital and attention with confidence, whether to one engine or several simultaneously. That comparison is an internal-data question, not a public-evidence one.
